How to Play
Puerto Rican Dominoes

Six steps to the table

The game, start to finish

Gather your set & your teams

  • Grab a double-six set — 28 tiles (fichas), running from the double-blank up to the double-six.
  • Four players form two teams of two. Partners sit across from each other, never side by side.
  • Any flat surface works. A picnic table is perfect.

Shuffle & deal

  • Turn every tile face down and swirl them around to mix — the table calls this dar agua ("giving them water").
  • Each player takes 7 tiles and stands them up so only they can see them.
  • All 28 get dealt — there's no draw pile. What you're holding is what you've got to work with.

The opening move (la salida)

  • First round of the match: whoever holds the double-six (6 | 6) lays it down to begin.
  • After that, the winner of the last round opens the next one and may lead any tile.
  • Play passes one seat at a time in the same direction around the table. Pick a direction and keep it all game.

Take turns matching the ends

  • On your turn, add one tile to either open end of the line, matching number to number — a 5 touches a 5.
  • Doubles are usually set crosswise, but they still just match their number.
  • Can't match either end? You pass — knock the table once (paso) and play moves on.
  • You can't draw and you can't skip for fun: if you have a legal play, you must play it.

Win the round

  • ¡Dominó! — the first player to lay down their last tile wins the round for their team.
  • Locked board (tranque): if nobody can play and the line is stuck, everyone flips their tiles and counts the pips left in hand. The team with the fewer total pips wins the round.

Keep score to 200

  • The winning team scores the total pips left in both opponents' hands.
  • First team to 200 points takes the match. (Plenty of families play to 100 — agree before you start.)
  • Hold the other team at zero for the whole match and you've handed them a zapato — bragging rights for the rest of the picnic.

Say it at the table

Talk like a boricua

La salida
The opening play that starts a round.
Paso
Knocking when you can't match — you pass.
Tranque
A locked board nobody can play on.
Capicú
Winning with a tile that fit on either end — the flashiest way to close.
Data
Feeding a number you know the other team can't follow.
Zapato
A shutout — the other team never scored a point.

Hold your own

Table-smart tips

  • Count what's down. Keep a rough tally of how many of each number have been played — it tells you what's still hiding in hands.
  • Read your partner's passes. When they knock on a number, don't reopen that end for the opponents, and lean toward the suits your partner keeps feeding.
  • Don't get stuck with a heavy double. Holding the 6 | 6 with few other sixes? Play it early before it strands you.
  • Keep both ends friendly. Try to leave the line showing numbers you can still answer next turn.
  • Your face is a tile too. Every pass leaks information to the whole table — including the other team.

Settle the house rules first. Every family plays a little differently — the target score (100 vs 200), whether a capicú earns bonus points, and exactly how a locked board scores. Agree on it before the first tile drops and save yourself the picnic argument.
